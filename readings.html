Reading Reflections

Reflection 1

Susan Sontag says that because photography is now a mass art form, it “is not practiced by most people as an art. It is mainly a social rite, a defense against anxiety, and a tool of power” (4). I would say these are accurate descriptions of how I use photography in my everyday life. Every time I hang out with my friends in person, my parents expect me to take a group photo with them. It’s like if I don’t take pictures, it didn’t happen. Sontag says that “To take a photograph is to participate in another person's (or thing's) mortality, vulnerability, mutability,” and I guess that’s why I feel the need to memorialize images of my loved ones and the time I spend with them—it lets me feel a little less anxious about inevitably losing them. I still wish I had more photos of my grandparents before they passed away, and I once asked my sister if I could take a picture of her just so I’d be able to remember her face. The same reason is also why I spam my camera on vacation. Sontag ascribes the impulse to take pictures as a tourist as a way “to take possession of a space in which [I am] insecure,” but for me it’s not about physical distance—it’s temporal (4). I fear forgetting these experiences, and feel obligated to take pictures of every special moment so I can look at them later and remember the good times. Photography is my defense against forgetfulness and the powerlessness I associate with it. It’s why I resorted to taking photos of the lecture slides in my New Media course last semester, because I didn’t trust my notetaking abilities to be adequate. But image making also functioned as a way to connect to my peers in that context, because I shared these pictures on the group chat so that all of us could survive a course where the slides don’t get posted and the exam questions are awful. I send my friends photos of silly things I see for the same purpose—so they can share in the experience of laughing at funny vanity plates or ugly McMansions, bringing us closer even when we can’t be physically together.

In terms of how I could visually represent notions of our world at the moment, I think I want to use images and text to highlight the mundane. I think there’s a lot of parts of our daily lives that are really absurd—whether in a lighthearted or fucked up way—when you actually take the time to think about them, but we take them for granted and don’t look at them. I want to create an interpretation of the world that places its frame on those overlooked things, because my viewer will find significance in my choice of subject simply for the fact that it is in a photograph and therefore somehow worth capturing. For instance, I’ve always been used to how uneven the sidewalks in Forest Hills are, but I think if I showed someone a photo of them with the caption “There’s A Retirement Home In My Neighborhood,” maybe they would think about how hilariously inaccessible these giant cracks are to anyone (e.g. the elderly) in a wheelchair.

Reflection 2

First of all, I do have to take issue with Malik’s phrasing. In her introduction to the article’s topic, Mailk contrasts “real pictures and footage of the world as it is” with “AI slop, low-quality content.” First of all, AI images and videos are not necessarily “low-quality”—they have become increasingly realistic as generative AI becomes more advanced, eliminating some of the more obvious tells like extra fingers. The assumption that AI slop is low-quality is a dangerous one, because it lets people assume that they can tell on sight if something is real, rather than encouraging them to actually question the content and information. Not to mention, this dichotomy also implies that human-made pictures and footage can be trusted to be accurate representations of reality, when this entire class is about teaching us how to manipulate pictures to make them look different. You don’t need AI to Photoshop someone into your Twitter post.

Beyond that, I find that I agree with Malik’s points in general. I don’t have much experience with political AI content, but I have noticed how it exploits the attention economy. A lot of YouTube channels use AI to make crossover videos, putting characters from one media property in another. There’s also AI voice generators that get people’s attention by letting you listen to someone, real or fictional, say something funny and/or offensive or sing a song or whatever (e.g. Ronald Reagan saying “I love the gays”). In both cases, the central conceit is dangling popular franchises and celebrities in front of people’s faces like jingling keys at a baby, and I’m ashamed to say it’s worked several times on me.

Beyond being attention-grabbing, using generative AI is just…easier (or at least it appears to be). Take the description of this fan song,: “I hopped on Suno and speed-wrote a song based on her character in like 15 minutes, and after some messing around with different versions of music pumped out by the AI, I spliced together a few to create this self-declared masterpiece.” Where creating music usually takes hours to days, generative AI offers to let you pump out a “masterpiece” in minutes. Which, of course, leads to nonsense like Netflix trying to get voice actors to agree to let their voices be fed to AI that will be used in movies and TV instead of them, because god forbid we have to pay real people when we can have the computer do it instead. Again, I’ve succumbed to the lure of using AI because it’s convenient—I used it for a project in my Anthropology class, because the professor literally encouraged us to and I was busy. And god knows how many times my eyes have instinctively gone to the AI summary at the top of a Google search, because it seemingly puts all the answers in one easy place.

Finally, I guess AI is contributing to the unrealness and overwhelming desensitization of our visual palates. However, as I said earlier, the existing human capacity for manipulatively editing and altering images was already potent before AI. While it certainly exacerbated the problem immensely by making the tools easier to access and use for anybody, I don’t think stopping or regulating its usage would end our current misinformation crisis. Furthermore, I’ve found that a lot of the disorientation I’m experiencing these days is based on what actually is happening. As Malik put it, “In the real world, US politicians pose outside prison cages of deportees. Students at US universities are ambushed in the street and spirited away. People in Gaza burn alive.” AI slop doesn’t really feel that much more absurd and insane than our terrible reality, where we are bombarded nonstop with over the top evils that go unpunished and unsolved.

Reflection 3

I found Motherhood in Crisis to be an interesting and educational experience overall. I like how they approached the maternity crisis from different angles such as the lack of resources in rural communities, the struggle of teen mothers, and the misogynistic justice system. I appreciate that, despite highlighting how much more helpful proper healthcare is for these mothers than traditional healers, these comics don’t judge them for not trusting hospitals considering the context of the Ebola crisis. I overall found the graphic novel to be very empathetic towards these women without veering into tragedy porn.

In terms of how it functions as an interactive story, I confess that my feelings on that are more mixed. I like the format of clicking through the pages. It’s the classic way webcomics are formatted because it lets you have a lot of content without forcing the reader to scroll through panels and panels. It was used especially effectively in Tetteh’s story, where you go from a panel of Tetteh happily watching her son play in the prison yard to a panel of her sitting alone staring at where he used to be. The abruptness of the transition really captures the suddenness of Alie being ripped away from her, and the reader can’t see it coming because it’s not on the same page as the previous panel.

However, there were some other choices that made less sense to me. You go to the next page by clicking the arrow, but also the page automatically changes on its own after a short time, which you only know by noticing the tiny bar at the top of the window fill up. I got ambushed by that at first and it was really jarring. I don’t get what the point is of automatically moving on like that if you’re also giving the reader control; I feel like they should have just done one or the other. It’s especially annoying because there’s also an animated component of text bubbles that only show up after a few seconds, and it was really annoying waiting for them to pop in. There didn’t seem to be any purpose to hiding the text bubbles at first; it didn’t add anything to the narrative or my experience as a reader. It wasn’t like they revealed anything particularly shocking that made me rethink my understanding of the current panel. All it did was make me click back to the previous page several times because I realized too late “oh wait, there was a second text bubble on that page.” Again, it feels like there’s a clash between letting the reader go at their own pace and controlling the speed of progression. Going back to that first point about the progress bar, I wish there was an easier way of going through the pages than one at a time. There’s different segments for each page of the story, so you’d think that clicking on them would let you skip ahead/back to the corresponding page, but there’s no interactivity there.

Reflection 4

Welp, there’s my daily dose of despair and anxiety about the state of this rotten world! It is deeply messed up how at any moment, one mistake could trigger a nuclear war that causes a full-blown apocalypse, and there is nothing we can do to counteract that possibility except pray the dumbasses in power keep their egos in check. I’ve been taking an economics class, and we’ve been talking about externalities and how it’s so hard to get people to agree to internalize the social costs of production because everyone wants someone else to lose out while they get to keep profiting. This is the same kind of problem—the actual solution is to just destroy all these damn nukes, but nobody wants to go through with that because if they do it first, then someone else might go back on their word and launch their nukes unopposed. The safety of the entire world hinges on a goddamn Prisoner’s Dilemma, except instead of two people there’s nine countries with apocalypse bombs. Also even if we got rid of the existing nuclear weapons, the genie is already out of the bottle and we can’t guarantee more won’t be made.

The interactive elements were very effective. The animated illustration of what a nuclear impact would look like was genuinely haunting in a way that still images wouldn’t be. Something about making you scroll through it makes it worse. It is also deeply harrowing to realize that the height of the nuclear stockpile was as recent as the 1980s. I thought the disarmament had begun in the 1960s, but the dots just kept increasing and increasing. I wish that the video segments had a progress bar and/or play button, because they would often start while I was still reading the text above them and make me wait for them to finish and loop in order to catch what was said at the beginning of them.

What I couldn’t help but notice was the political bias in this article. The author highlights the threat of nuclear strikes from India, Pakistan, North Korea, and China…but the non-Russian countries with the most nukes are the US, Britain, and France. Furthermore, the author stresses that “Iran, which has continued its nuclear program amid Israel’s war on Hamas in Gaza, has amassed enough enriched uranium to build several weapons if and when it chooses” and “A nuclear explosion in Ukraine or Gaza […] would sizeably escalate either conflict and its humanitarian toll.” This implies that the real danger to Palestine is Iran’s hypothetical nukes and not Israel’s actual nukes (enough to demarcate it as a nuclear superpower) being launched at the country they are currently genociding. It feels like the author is playing on Orientalist anxieties about the Scary Evil Asians throwing around nukes in power grabs that the Calm and Good Westerners are preventing with their Rationalism and Diplomacy. If the Orientals have nuclear weapons, it’s an existential threat to the world. If the Occidentals have nuclear weapons, it is a defensive measure that they are only resorting to because of those Scary Evil Asians.
