Photomontage


"Divorce Wins"

In this photomontage, I’m trying to explore my personal feelings about being aromantic and asexual in a society that stigmatizes both of these orientations. In order to express how much being aroace colors my experiences, I decided to subvert and reinterpret conventional romantic imagery while combining it with symbols of aromanticism. I knew I wanted to use a picture of Eros/Cupid, the Greek/Roman God of Love co-opted into the sickening cherub of Valentine’s Day, and the first result that came up when I searched “cupid” on Wikimedia Commons was William Adoph-Bougureau's A Girl Defending Herself Against Eros showing a girl playfully defending herself from Eros. I liked Eros’ pose of basically shanking this girl and how the girl reflected my relationship to the concept of romance—I like to play with it, but it still has to stay the hell away from me. My second image is the cover of Elizabeth Brake's Minimizing Marriage: Marriage, Morality and the Law, sourced from Wikipedia. Despite what the bride-and-groom topper on the cover might suggest, this is the book that coined the term amatonormativity, a.k.a “the widespread assumption that everyone is better off in an exclusive, romantic, long-term coupled relationship, and that everyone is seeking such a relationship,” a.k.a the bane of my existence. My third image is an arrow pin with the feathers representing the colors of the aromantic pride flag. I realized that the aro/arrow pun works really well with a subverted Eros, turning the arrows he uses to force romantic feelings on people into symbols of rejecting the romance forced on us by society. Finally, I decided to use an aroace flag for the blending, because I figured its colors would work better than the aromantic or asexual flags and I am both of these things at once.

Once I chose the cover of Minimizing Marriage and the arrow pin, I hit upon the idea of replacing Eros’ arrow with the aro arrow pin, then posing him and the wedding topper so that it looked like Eros was throwing the arrow at the bride and groom, thus bestowing the gift of aromanticism upon the artificial construct of heteronormativity. To accomplish this, I selected Eros’ arrow with the polygonal lasso and used an inverted mask to hide it. I then put the layer with the aro arrow pin on top of the Bougureau painting, used a mask around the pin to hide the jeans and make it look two-dimensional and flat, and moved it on top of Eros’ hand where the original arrow was. In order to get the book cover in the picture, I put the pin and painting layers in a group, applied a layer mask to that group, and fiddled with the gradient tool. The Angle Gradient proved to be the best option because Eros and the wedding topper were in opposite corners and it let me keep the book’s title faintly visible. By increasing the feather, I made the grass of the painting and cover blend together more seamlessly (not to mention managed to use the groom’s head to cover the breasts). Next, I put the aroace flag on top of all the other layers, rotated it vertically, and used the Soft Light Blending Mode to combine everything together; it was the only one that I felt didn’t overwhelm the image too much, giving it a distinctive tint of the flag’s colors without drowning out its own. However, it was hard to make out the details of the scene with the solid colors overlaid, so I dialed down the opacity of the flag layer to 74%. Finally, I adjusted the exposure a little bit with Curves to brighten the shadows and keep the text visible.
